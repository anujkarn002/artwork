The Art of Pochampally Ikat Weaving

Watch Lakshmi Devi demonstrate the traditional tie-dye technique

I was born in Pochampally village, known for its unique geometric patterns in Ikat weaving. At the age of 12, I began learning this craft from my mother, who herself was a master weaver. Our family has been involved in this tradition for over five generations.

The most challenging aspect was learning to tie the yarn in precise patterns before dyeing - a process that requires immense patience and precision. Each mistake means starting over, and in my early days, I would often spend days redoing the same section.

Preserving Heritage

In the 1980s, I saw many young people from our village abandoning weaving for factory jobs in cities. That's when I decided to not only practice but teach and preserve our heritage. With support from the local handicraft board, I started training young women in our community.

Over the years, I've trained more than 50 artisans, many of whom now run their own weaving enterprises. Seeing them succeed gives me immense satisfaction. In 1994, I received my first National Award for a complex double Ikat saree that took six months to complete.

Innovations in Tradition

While I respect traditional patterns, I've also experimented with contemporary designs and color combinations to appeal to younger customers. I believe tradition survives when it adapts.

Today, my work has reached international exhibitions, and I take pride in sharing our cultural heritage with the world. Each piece I create carries centuries of knowledge and the identity of our community.

"The threads we weave connect us to our ancestors and to future generations. Each knot, each color tells our story."

The Craft Process

1

Design Preparation

Creating the pattern on graph paper before transferring it to the yarn. This determines the final design.

2

Tie-Dye Process

Bundling and tying sections of yarn with waterproof material before dyeing to create the pattern.

3

Weaving

Carefully aligning the dyed yarns on the loom and weaving them together to reveal the final pattern.
